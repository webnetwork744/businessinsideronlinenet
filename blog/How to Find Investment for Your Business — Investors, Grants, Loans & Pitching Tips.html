7-step plan to find investors

1 — Validate: Get early customers or pre-sales to show demand.
2 — Prepare: 1-page executive summary, 5-slide pitch, and financial snapshot.
3 — Target: Build a list of 50 relevant investors (stage, sector, geography).
4 — Warm intros: Use LinkedIn, mutual connections, and events to get introductions.
5 — Outreach: Send concise emails with traction + ask for 15-min call.
6 — Follow-up: Track replies, send updates, and close meetings.
7 — Negotiate: Understand term sheets, valuation, and preferred investor terms before accepting offers.

Detailed funding routes

  • Friends & Family: Fast but document the terms to avoid disputes.
  • Pre-sales & Revenue: Use early customer revenue to fund growth and reduce dilution.
  • Angel syndicates: Group investments that can write checks quickly.
  • Strategic corporate investors: Partnerships that bring distribution and capital.
  • Crowdfunding: Great for product-market validation and raising demand-led capital.
  • Revenue-based financing: Non-dilutive option repaid from a share of revenue.
  • Bank loans & microloans: Consider for founders with collateral or predictable cash flow.
  • Grant programs: Sector-specific non-dilutive opportunities (tech, green, social impact).

Pitch & Outreach Examples (Best Practices)

Lead with traction, keep emails short (50–80 words), attach a 1-sentence ask, and always include a single KPI that proves momentum. Use warm intros—cold emails have lower success rates.

FAQ

When should I approach investors?

Approach angels for early traction; VCs typically expect product-market fit and repeatable growth. Use pre-sales or pilot customers to build credibility.

How many investors should I contact?

Start with a focused list of 30–50 targeted investors and aim for warm introductions. Quality > quantity.

What documents do investors expect?

A one-page executive summary, a 5–10 slide deck, a simple financial model, and references or customer proof if available.
